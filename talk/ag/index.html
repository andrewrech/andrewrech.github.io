Antigen garnish: ensemble neoepitope prediction from DNA variants

Abstract

The immune system exerts anti-tumor activity via T cell-dependent recognition of tumor-specific antigens. Although the number of tumor neoepitopes – peptides derived from somatic mutations – often correlates with immune activity and survival, most classically-defined high affinity neoepitopes (CDNs) are not immunogenic, and only rare CDNs have been linked to tumor rejection. Thus, the rules of tumor antigen recognition remain incompletely understood. We analyzed neoepitopes, immune activity, and clinical outcome from 6,324 patients across 27 tumor types. We characterized a class of ‘alternatively defined neoepitopes’ (ADNs), which are mutant peptides predicted to bind MHC (class I or II) with greatly improved affinity relative to their non-mutated counterpart. ADNs are abundant and molecularly distinct from CDNs. The load of ADNs correlates with intratumoral T cell response and immune suppression. Moreover, ADNs are strong predictors of patient survival across tumor types. To facilitate the study of ADNs, we also report a thorough, simple, and fast cancer immunotherapy program called antigen.garnish for ensemble identification of priority ADNs and CDNs. antigen.garnish implements 7 existing neoepitope prediction algorithms on peptides derived from human or murine DNA missense mutations, insertions, deletions, and gene fusions. Neoepitopes are ranked by MHC I/II binding affinity, dissimilarity to the normal peptidome, and similarity to known immunogenic antigens. Results for 1000 variants are ranked in a single step in less than five minutes. Our identification of ADNs and antigen.garnish expand the spectrum of mutation-derived tumor antigens with potential clinical relevance. antigen.garnish is an open source R package available for all platforms at http://neoepitopes.io.
